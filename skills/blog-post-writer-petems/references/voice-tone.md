# Peter's Voice and Tone

Captured from analysing posts at petersouter.xyz

> Based on the excellent content creation skills by Nick Nisi.
> Original: <https://github.com/nicknisi/claude-plugins/tree/main/plugins/content>
> This is my own personal take on that flow, tuned to my voice and preferences.

## Core Writing Characteristics

### Voice

Casual, self-deprecating, British. Peter writes like he's sat in a pub telling you about something that happened at work, occasionally pausing to go "wait, I should back up a bit." There's warmth and honesty throughout, but never polish for polish's sake.

### Vulnerability

Peter doesn't just admit mistakes, he leads with them. Forgot the draft flag on a blog post? That's the opening paragraph. Missed a conference deadline? He'll tell you exactly how it happened. Scope creep ate the project? He'll walk you through it with a rueful grin.

Examples:

- "sheepishly write another navel-gazing blogpost"
- "dance-between-the-raindrops approach to deadlines"
- "brings me out in hives"
- Openly admitting when something took way longer than expected

### Narrative Structure

Posts meander (and Peter knows it). He'll go on a tangent about vexillology or the bear on the Zheleznogorsk flag, then pull himself back with something like "anyway, the point is..." The structure is less "setup, confrontation, resolution" and more "let me tell you about this thing, oh and also
this other thing, and actually they connect like this."

### Sentence Style

- Parenthetical asides everywhere (mid-sentence thoughts in parens are a signature move)
- Bold for rallying cries: **DO THE DAMN THING**, **Boom, one deadline down**
- Trailing ellipsis when a thought drifts off...
- Mix of short punchy lines and longer rambling ones
- Fragments for emphasis. Absolutely.
- British spellings: favourite, realised, colour, practise (verb)

### Specific Phrases and Patterns

**Opening hooks:**

- Often starts by undercutting himself or the topic
- "So I finally got around to..."
- Self-aware about the fact he's writing yet another blog post
- Sets up with a personal anecdote before getting to the tech

**Transitions:**

- "Anyway, the point is..."
- "So here's the thing..."
- "Right, so..."
- "Which brings me to..."
- "(I promise this is going somewhere)"

**Emphasis techniques:**

- **Bold text** for key rallying points and emphasis
- Parenthetical asides for colour commentary
- Emojis used sparingly and conversationally (😆 🤔), never decoratively
- Italics for gentle stress or self-aware moments

**Humour and references:**

- Niche passion tangents: vexillology, Zheleznogorsk bears, baseball (Terrance Gore), powerlifting
- Self-aware about the tangents ("I know, I know, bear with me")
- Knowing winks to the reader
- British understatement: "not ideal" when something was catastrophic

### Technical Writing Style

**Code and tools:**

- DevOps/infrastructure native: Puppet, Terraform, Docker, Kubernetes, Vault
- Shows actual commands, configs, and output
- Explains context without over-explaining the basics
- Drops specific version numbers, error messages, and real outputs
- Uses inline code formatting naturally: `puppet apply`, `terraform plan`, `vault write`

**Examples:**

- Real error messages and stack traces
- Actual config snippets from production (sanitised)
- Links to specific PRs, issues, and commits
- Conference names and people mentioned by name (decade-long community relationships)

### Honesty About Limitations

Peter is upfront about the messy bits:

- "This took me way longer than it should have"
- "I'm still not 100% sure this is the right approach"
- Admits when he's writing about something he's still learning
- Calls out his own scope creep and deadline misses

### Self-Awareness and Humour

Light, self-deprecating, never punching down:

- Knows when he's rambling and calls it out
- References to being a "navel-gazing" blogger
- Acknowledges his niche interests with a wink
- Comfortable being the butt of the joke
- Never tries to sound smarter than he is

### Structure Patterns

**Sections often use:**

- Descriptive headers that are specific to the content
- Lists with real examples
- Code blocks with actual output
- Links to external resources and references
- Trip reports and conference recaps full of personal context

**Common section patterns:**

- The personal hook into the technical content
- "Here's what actually happened" honest retrospective
- Community context: who he talked to, what was discussed
- Practical takeaway at the end

### Conclusions

Endings often:

- Circle back to the opening anecdote or theme
- Include a practical takeaway or call to action
- End on a self-aware note about the post itself
- Forward-looking but honest about uncertainty

Examples:

- Rallying cry: **DO THE DAMN THING**
- Self-deprecating closer about writing more regularly
- Practical "here's what I'd recommend" wrap-up
- Acknowledgement that he went on too long (with a wink)

### Authenticity Markers

**Real examples:**

- Specific conference names, talks, and attendees
- Actual tool versions, error messages, and configs
- Real project names and outcomes
- Personal life woven in naturally (kiddos, cake, Gabby's Playhouse)

**Community embedded:**

- Knows conference folks by first name
- References decade-long relationships in the DevOps/Puppet community
- Trip reports that are as much about the people as the tech
- Genuine enthusiasm for the community, not performative

### Tone Shifts

Peter modulates tone based on content:

- **Technical deep-dives**: Detailed, specific, still conversational
- **Conference recaps**: Warm, community-focused, anecdotal
- **Personal reflections**: Vulnerable, self-deprecating, honest
- **Tool walkthroughs**: Practical, shows real commands, admits pain points

### What to Avoid

- Corporate speak or marketing language
- Polished perfection (the mess is part of the charm)
- Em dashes (use commas, periods, or parentheses instead)
- American English spellings
- Hiding uncertainty or pretending to have all the answers
- Being preachy or condescending
- Forced humour (it should feel natural, not workshopped)
- Over-engineering the structure (let it meander a bit)

### Key Principles

1. Be casual but substantive
2. Lead with the personal, connect to the technical
3. Admit when you're uncertain, wrong, or late
4. Use specific details: tool names, version numbers, real people
5. Parenthetical asides are your friend (seriously, use them)
6. Bold for emphasis and rallying cries, not for formatting
7. British English, always
8. Emojis sparingly, conversationally, never decoratively
9. It's okay to ramble if you're aware you're doing it
10. The reader is a peer, not a student
